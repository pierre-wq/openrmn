How openRMN works

The independent retail media analytics layer, explained end-to-end. From OAuth connection to AI-powered insights — every step of the pipeline, in plain English.

Reading time 8 min Last updated 2026-04-15 Open methodology — all formulas auditable on GitHub

Meet "Maison Dubois", a coffee advertiser

Let's ground the explanation in a real-world case. Maison Dubois is a French premium coffee brand. They sell 4 SKUs: a Bialetti-style Italian coffee maker, a cast-iron teapot, an insulated mug, and organic coffee filters.

Their products are listed on Amazon, Cdiscount (Criteo Retail Media) and Carrefour.fr (Unlimitail). They spend €50k/month on retail media, split across the three networks.

Today, their marketing team spends two full days per month exporting three different Excel files, manually normalizing them, and trying to understand which campaigns are actually working. The data doesn't align: Amazon reports in ASINs, Criteo in GTINs, Unlimitail in their own SKU format. Attribution windows differ. Metrics use different names for the same thing. This is the exact problem openRMN solves.

The pipeline at a glance

Step 1 — Connecting your retail media accounts

When Maison Dubois signs into openRMN for the first time, they're guided through connecting their three retail media accounts. For each network, the flow is the same:

  1. Click "Connect" on the Amazon Ads card.
  2. Get redirected to amazon.com, where they log in with their regular Amazon Advertising credentials.
  3. Amazon asks: "Do you authorize openRMN to read your advertising performance data?" They click Yes.
  4. They're redirected back to openRMN, now connected. Takes 20 seconds.
What actually happens under the hood

Amazon returns a refresh token — a long-lived credential that openRMN stores encrypted in the server's .env file. No password is ever stored. openRMN can now query the Amazon Ads API 24/7 on behalf of Maison Dubois, strictly read-only, strictly on their account.

Maison Dubois can revoke access at any time from their Amazon Ads console, killing the refresh token instantly. Same pattern for Criteo and Unlimitail.

Every night at 3 AM, openRMN calls the three APIs and requests the last 30 days of performance data. Here's what each network actually returns.

Same product. Same day. Three completely different formats. Amazon calls its identifier an "ASIN", Criteo calls it a "GTIN". Amazon says "cost", Criteo says "spend". Amazon attributes sales over 7 days, Criteo over 30 days. This is the chaos openRMN translates.

Step 3 — Normalization (the heart of the product)

openRMN takes these three formats and translates them into a single schema we call UnifiedRow. Each row represents "the performance of one product on one network on one day":

@dataclass
class UnifiedRow:
    date: str                    # ISO format
    rmn: str                     # "amazon" | "criteo" | "unlimitail"
    retailer: str                # amazon.fr, cdiscount.com, ...
    campaign_id: str
    campaign_name: str
    sku: str                     # ASIN, GTIN, or internal SKU
    product_name: str            # Normalized product name
    impressions: int
    clicks: int
    spend_eur: float
    units_sold: int
    sales_eur: float
    new_to_brand_units: int

The same product now has comparable rows across networks. It's the equivalent of putting every network into the same currency and the same language. Every subsequent calculation — KPIs, anomaly detection, trust scoring, double-counting audit — operates on this unified schema.

Step 4 — Matching products across networks

This is the most technically delicate question in cross-network retail media analytics: how do you know that Amazon's B07ABCD123 and Criteo's 3700987654321 refer to the same physical product?

openRMN supports three levels of matching, from simplest to most rigorous.

Level 1 — Product name matching (MVP default)

We normalize product_name (lowercase, strip accents, remove articles like "the" and "la") and match by string similarity. Works in 60-70% of cases for a clean catalog. Fragile: "Bialetti Italian coffee maker" vs "Bialetti 6-cup coffee maker Premium" won't match.

Level 2 — EAN/GTIN matching (recommended for production)

The EAN (European Article Number, 13 digits) is a universal identifier. The same barcode 3700987654321 identifies the same physical product on Amazon, Cdiscount and Carrefour. This is the clean solution.

The catch: Amazon does not systematically expose the EAN in its API (it exposes the proprietary ASIN instead). openRMN requires the advertiser to provide a lookup table — a simple CSV from their PIM (Product Information Management system):

ean,asin,criteo_sku,unlimitail_sku,internal_reference
3700987654321,B07ABCD123,CRT-4455,UNL-9988,BIALETTI-6T
3700987654322,B07ABCE456,CRT-4456,UNL-9989,TETSUBIN-ART
3700987654323,B07ABCE789,CRT-4457,UNL-9990,THERMOS-500
3700987654324,B07ABCEFGH,CRT-4458,UNL-9991,BIOFILTER-100

A 10-minute task for the e-commerce team, because this table already exists in their PIM. From that point on, every join is clean and unambiguous.

Level 3 — AI-powered fuzzy matching

For advertisers who cannot provide a mapping table, openRMN can leverage a sentence embedding model (e.g. sentence-transformers) to semantically understand that "Bialetti Italian coffee maker 6 cups" and "Bialetti Moka Express Premium 6 cups" likely refer to the same product. Cosine similarity above 0.85 triggers an auto-match, between 0.70 and 0.85 prompts the user for confirmation.

Step 5 — Centralizing data on a single product

When the marketing director of Maison Dubois clicks on "Bialetti Italian coffee maker" in openRMN, here's what the tool does in under one second:

  1. Filter the unified DataFrame on product_name = "Bialetti Italian coffee maker" (or ean = "3700987654321" in v2).
  2. Retrieve all matching rows — typically 3 networks × 14 days = 42 rows.
  3. Aggregate per network:
    • Amazon: €2,047 spent, €7,851 attributed sales, 4.16x ROAS
    • Criteo: €892 spent, €3,420 attributed sales, 3.83x ROAS
    • Unlimitail: €456 spent, €2,107 attributed sales, 4.62x ROAS
  4. Compute derived metrics (CTR, CPC, units sold, new-to-brand share).
  5. Run anomaly detection on this specific product.
  6. Run the double-counting audit: the sum of declared sales is €13,378 but estimated real sales are only €9,200 — meaning €4,178 of over-attribution (31%).
  7. Pass everything to the AI agent, which writes a narrative brief in the advertiser's language.

Step 6 — KPIs, anomalies and trust scores

Before the AI agent is even called, a deterministic Python layer (pandas + pure functions) computes everything that must be verifiable and reproducible. This is critical: the LLM never invents numbers. It only interprets what the deterministic layer has already computed.

Unified KPIs

Simple aggregates: total spend, total attributed sales, unified ROAS, breakdown per network. All computed with pandas groupby operations. Full code in agent.py, function compute_kpis().

Anomaly detection

Two rule-based detectors in v0.5:

Cross-network cannibalization: when the same product shows ROAS values that diverge significantly between two networks (ratio > 1.8 or < 0.55), flag it. A potential signal that one network is cannibalizing another, or that attribution methodologies differ enough to produce misleading numbers.

Underperforming campaigns: any campaign with > €5,000 spend and ROAS < 1.5 is flagged as underperforming.

Trust Score per network

For each network, a 0-100 score based on 4 weighted components:

  • Internal consistency (30%): variance of daily ROAS
  • Cross-network convergence (25%): correlation with other networks on common SKUs
  • Methodology transparency (25%): whether the network publicly documents its attribution window and logic
  • Data freshness (20%): lag between measurement and API availability

All formulas are open and auditable in agent.py → function trust_score().

Double-counting audit

For each shared SKU, estimates "real" sales as max(sales_per_network) × 1.1 (a defensible heuristic: the most aggressive network is likely closest to the truth, plus 10% for sales outside of advertised impressions). The difference between summed declared sales and estimated real sales is the over-attribution amount, visualized as a Sankey diagram.

Function: double_counting_audit().

Methodology harmonization simulator

Every network uses a different attribution window (Amazon 7d, Criteo 30d, Unlimitail 14d). The simulator rescales each network's attributed sales using the rule coefficient = sqrt(target_window / actual_window) and shows before/after KPIs. Gives the advertiser an answer to "what would my dashboard look like if everyone followed the same rules?".

Step 7 — The AI brief layer

The AI agent (Claude Sonnet 4.5) never touches the raw data directly. It receives a structured payload containing:

{
  "selection_scope": "Full portfolio — 4 products, 3 networks, 14 days",
  "aggregated_kpis": { ... all computed numbers ... },
  "anomalies_detected": [ ... cannibalization + underperforming ... ],
  "trust_scores": { "amazon": 78, "criteo": 65, "unlimitail": 71 },
  "double_counting_audit": {
    "total_attributed": 458200,
    "estimated_real": 320500,
    "overlap_amount": 137700,
    "overlap_pct": 43.0
  }
}

Depending on the persona selected (Executive, Operational, or Auditor), the system prompt changes entirely, producing three completely different briefs from the same underlying data. For free-form Q&A, the user's question is appended to the same payload with a generalist analyst system prompt.

Streaming is via Server-Sent Events: the brief appears in the sidebar token by token, just like ChatGPT.

Why this architecture matters

Numbers come from pandas. Interpretation comes from Claude. This separation is deliberate: every figure in the brief can be cross-checked against the deterministic layer. The AI cannot hallucinate KPIs because it doesn't compute them — it only narrates what has already been calculated.

MCP server — talk to openRMN from any AI agent

openRMN exposes its deterministic analytics layer as a Model Context Protocol (MCP) server. Any MCP-compatible AI agent — Claude Desktop, Claude Code, Cursor, custom agents — can query retail media metrics in natural language, without writing a line of code.

This is what makes openRMN composable. The analytics layer is no longer locked inside a dashboard: it's a tool any agent can call, anywhere in your stack.

Example interaction (Claude Desktop)

User: "My Q2 retail media dashboard shows 458k in attributed sales. My CFO doesn't trust that number. Can you give me a cleaner estimate?"

Claude (calling openRMN via MCP): "Running the double-counting audit... your networks report 458k combined, but the de-duplicated estimate is 320k — meaning 138k (43%) is probably over-attributed noise. The biggest overlap is on 'Cafetiere Italienne Inox 6 tasses' where both Amazon and Criteo claim nearly-identical sales volumes."

The numbers Claude quotes come from openRMN's Python layer, not the model's imagination. Full technical setup in the GitHub repository.

Why this matters strategically

The advertising industry is moving toward agentic execution (see AdCP — the emerging Ad Context Protocol). In that future, multiple AI agents coordinate: one plans, one buys, one creates assets. openRMN positions itself as the measurement and audit agent — the one that tells the others "that number you're optimizing against is 43% over-attributed."

Being MCP-native today means openRMN is ready for the agentic advertising stack that every serious industry observer expects to emerge in the next 18-24 months.

Privacy by design & deployment options

Retail media data is sensitive. Campaign budgets, attributed sales, product margins, customer acquisition costs — this is information your CFO would not want competitors, ad networks, or even your own SaaS vendors to see in aggregate. openRMN is designed around this constraint from day one.

Three deployment modes for three risk profiles

Unlike proprietary SaaS competitors (Skai, Improvado, Pacvue, Mimbi), openRMN is open source and can be deployed in three different modes depending on how much control you want over your data.

  1. Self-hosted (most secure)

    You run openRMN on your own infrastructure — a server in your datacenter, a VPC in your AWS/GCP/Azure account, or even a Raspberry Pi in your office. Your retail media data never leaves your network. The only outbound calls are to the retail networks' APIs (Amazon, Criteo, Unlimitail) which you already trust by definition. Recommended for large advertisers with internal data governance constraints.

  2. Private cloud (managed by your IT team)

    Deploy openRMN in a Docker container on your existing cloud infrastructure. Same isolation as self-hosted, with the convenience of cloud-native deployment. Compatible with Kubernetes, ECS, Cloud Run, Container Instances. A single Dockerfile is all you need — full deployment guide in the GitHub repository.

  3. Hosted demo (this lab.holco.co instance)

    Run by openRMN's maintainers for evaluation purposes only. Convenient for trying out the product without any setup, but not intended for production data. We strongly recommend self-hosting before connecting real campaign accounts.

What data leaves your server

This is the question to ask any analytics vendor. Here is the honest answer for openRMN:

  • To retail media networks (Amazon, Criteo, Unlimitail): OAuth authorization at setup time, then read-only API calls to fetch your own performance data. Same network calls as if you logged into their dashboards manually. No data flows from openRMN to these networks beyond the API requests themselves.
  • To Anthropic (Claude API): only when you click "Generate brief" or ask a free-form question. Aggregated KPIs (totals, averages, anomalies) are sent — never raw customer data, never shopper-level information, never PII. Anthropic's commercial terms contractually prohibit using API inputs for model training. If even this minimal exposure is unacceptable, the AI agent can be disabled entirely (set ANTHROPIC_API_KEY to empty) and openRMN continues to function as a deterministic analytics tool.
  • To anyone else: nothing. No analytics tracker, no telemetry, no usage stats sent to openRMN maintainers. We don't know who uses our software unless they tell us. This is a deliberate design choice.
Run it air-gapped if you want

If you self-host openRMN and disable the Claude integration, the only outbound network calls left are to the retail media networks' own APIs. You can audit this with a packet capture: openRMN makes zero calls to any third party beyond the networks you explicitly connected.

Authentication & secret management

OAuth refresh tokens and API credentials are stored in a server-side .env file with restrictive Unix permissions (chmod 600, owner-readable only). They never reach the browser, never appear in logs, never travel through any third party. The frontend interacts with the openRMN backend over a session cookie signed with HMAC-SHA256 using a per-deployment secret you control.

Tokens can be revoked at any time from the originating network's dashboard. openRMN holds no privileged credentials — it operates strictly under the read-only scope you grant during the OAuth flow.

GDPR & European data residency

openRMN itself does not collect or process personal data of end consumers. The data ingested via retail media APIs is aggregated campaign performance — impression counts, click counts, attributed sales totals — not individual shopper records. As such, openRMN's use does not introduce new GDPR processing obligations beyond those you already have with the retail networks themselves.

For French and European advertisers concerned about data residency: in self-hosted mode, all data stays on the server you choose. The only cross-border data flow is the optional Anthropic API call, for which Anthropic offers EU-resident processing options for enterprise customers — see Anthropic's commercial terms. Other LLM providers with EU-hosted infrastructure (Mistral, OVH AI Endpoints) can be swapped in by editing a single function in agent.py.

What we will never do

  • Sell, share, rent, or analyze your campaign data for any purpose other than serving you.
  • Train AI models on your retail media performance figures.
  • Add silent telemetry, usage analytics, or "phone home" calls to openRMN. The repository's network footprint is auditable line by line.
  • Lock you into a proprietary format. Every UnifiedRow can be exported to CSV, Parquet, or any DataFrame-compatible format with a single function call.
  • Charge you for accessing your own data. The open source product is free forever. Premium services (managed hosting, third-party panel data via Wakoopa, dedicated support) are separately priced and clearly identified.

What openRMN is NOT

Naming what a product does is easy. Naming what it deliberately doesn't do is more useful — it tells you whether the tool fits your workflow.

  • openRMN is not a DSP or a media buying tool. It does not buy ads, edit campaigns, change bids, or push budget changes back to the networks. It is read-only, by design. For activation and execution, look at tools like Concord, Pacvue, or the native interfaces of each network.
  • openRMN is not a tag manager or pixel-based tracker. It does not deploy JavaScript on your website, does not track individual shoppers, and does not build audience segments. Everything it sees comes from the official measurement APIs of each retail media network.
  • openRMN is not a replacement for an MMM or an MTA platform. Marketing Mix Modeling and Multi-Touch Attribution require longer historical data, channel-mix modeling, and statistical techniques outside the scope of this tool. openRMN focuses on the cross-network retail media problem specifically — it complements MMM rather than replaces it.
  • openRMN is not a CDP or a customer database. No PII is stored, no shopper-level data is processed. The smallest unit of analysis is a campaign × product × day row. Customer-level insights belong in your CDP.
  • openRMN is not a panel-based measurement provider. For "true" cross-network deduplicated sales (the answer to "what would my sales have been without ads at all?"), you need an independent panel like Wakoopa, NielsenIQ, Kantar Worldpanel, or Circana. openRMN can integrate with these panels as data sources to enrich its neutrality audit, but does not replace them.
  • openRMN is not a fully autonomous AI agent. The AI generates briefs and answers questions, but it does not take actions on your behalf. Every recommendation requires a human to act on it (in your media buying tool, your team, your budget process). This is a deliberate trust boundary — automation of analysis, not of decision.
Where openRMN sits in your stack

Think of openRMN as a measurement and audit layer between your retail media activation tools and your business reporting (Looker, Power BI, Tableau, your CFO's spreadsheet). It does one thing well: consolidate and audit. It deliberately does not try to do everything.

How we compare to existing tools

The retail media analytics space has several established players. Here is an honest positioning of openRMN against them. Where they are better, we say so.

vs Skai (formerly Kenshoo)

Skai is a mature, full-stack retail media platform with bid optimization, campaign management, and reporting in one suite. It serves enterprise advertisers and agencies with €50k+/year contracts. openRMN does not compete on activation features — Skai is far ahead there. We focus on the measurement and audit layer that Skai's own reporting cannot provide neutrally (since Skai is paid by the advertiser, but bids are placed inside the networks' walled gardens).

vs Improvado / Supermetrics / Dataslayer

These are excellent ETL connectors that pull retail media data into Google Sheets, BigQuery, or Snowflake. They do the data plumbing well. openRMN does the analytics layer that they leave to the user — anomaly detection, trust scoring, double-counting audit, AI briefs. You can use both: Improvado as the connector, openRMN as the analytics on top, if your team prefers that split.

vs Mimbi

Mimbi is the closest comparable: a retail media data warehouse with cross-network normalization. They are further along on production deployment and connector breadth. openRMN's differentiation is being open source (auditable code, no vendor lock-in) and shipping the AI-driven audit layer (trust scores, harmonization simulator, double-counting visualization) out of the box.

vs AudienceProject

AudienceProject is an independent cross-media measurement platform with a strong focus on broadcast TV, CTV, and walled-garden brand campaigns. They use panel-based measurement at scale. openRMN is more narrowly focused on retail media performance specifically and uses network-declared data (not panel data) as its primary source. The two approaches are complementary: AudienceProject for upper-funnel reach and brand impact, openRMN for lower-funnel retail media performance and attribution.

vs in-house spreadsheet workflows

This is openRMN's most direct alternative for mid-market advertisers today. The honest comparison: a well-maintained internal spreadsheet can do what openRMN does for KPIs and basic reporting. It cannot do the trust scoring, the cross-network audit, or the AI briefs without significant investment in data engineering and analyst time. openRMN removes the maintenance burden while opening up audit features that manual spreadsheets cannot replicate.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to onboard a new advertiser?

Thirty minutes. Ten for the three OAuth connections, ten for uploading the product mapping CSV, ten for the first data fetch to complete.

What happens if a retail media network changes its API tomorrow?

We maintain the connectors like any SaaS vendor would. The advantage of being open source: the community can contribute fixes. Same approach as Airbyte for data connectors or LangChain for LLM integrations.

How do you guarantee your "estimated real sales" are accurate?

Today, we don't — it's a documented and auditable heuristic (max per network × 1.1). Going further requires plugging in a third-party panel source (e.g. Wakoopa, NielsenIQ, Kantar Worldpanel). This is exactly where openRMN transitions from a free open-source tool to a premium service with an external source of truth.

Is my data shared with any third party?

The only third party that sees aggregated (non-PII) campaign data is Anthropic (Claude API), and only for the purpose of generating natural language briefs. No advertiser identifiers, no shopper-level data, no PII is ever transmitted. Anthropic's commercial terms prohibit using API inputs for model training.

Can I self-host openRMN?

Yes. The entire stack is open source and runs on any Linux server with Python 3.11+. Full deployment guide in the repository README.

Why open source?

Because "neutral third party" and "proprietary black box" are contradictions. If we claim to audit the walled gardens, our own code must be auditable too. Every Trust Score formula, every attribution harmonization coefficient, every anomaly detection rule is visible in the GitHub repo.

Can openRMN see my customers' personal data?

No. The retail media APIs return aggregated performance data (impressions, clicks, spend, attributed sales counts) — never individual shopper records. openRMN never receives, processes, or stores PII at any point in its pipeline. The smallest unit of data is one campaign × one product × one day.

What if I don't trust Anthropic with my data?

You can disable the AI agent entirely — set ANTHROPIC_API_KEY to empty and openRMN runs as a pure deterministic analytics tool. You keep all KPIs, anomaly detection, trust scores, and the double-counting audit. You only lose the natural-language briefs and free-form Q&A. Alternatively, swap in another LLM provider (Mistral hosted in France, OVH AI Endpoints, a local Ollama server on your own GPU) by editing one function in agent.py — the agent layer is intentionally provider-agnostic.
